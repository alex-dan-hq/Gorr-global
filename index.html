Five Mechanisms of Layer 2 Exposure
1. Squeezed Middle

Accountable for team performance and compliance without authority to hire, fire, discipline, or redirect pay. Creates learned helplessness and responsibility without agency.

2. Unpaid/Under-Recognized Responsibility

De facto store manager on quiet shifts; keyholder liability for loss, accident, or security breach. Night shift supervisors often solo-managing £500K+ store value with minimal support.

3. Technology Burden

Managing multiple systems (POS, ordering, scheduling, compliance, ESL, scanners) while customer-facing. Training gaps common; expectations of tech expertise without specialist support.

4. Administrative Creep

Compliance paperwork (food safety, H&S, accident reports, audits, training sign-offs) consuming 20–30% of time. No dedicated administrative support. Regulatory burden falls on supervisor.

5. Customer Abuse Frontline

First called to shoplifting, loud complaints, age-restricted disputes, refund requests. 54% reduction in violence with body-worn cameras. Abuse underreported; post-incident support often minimal.

Pay Compression Risk

When base pay rises faster than supervisor premium, the wage gap narrows. Co-op's 40p/hr premium was insufficient — supervisors earned only 15–20% above band 1 peers despite significantly higher responsibility. National Minimum Wage rise (April 2026) may compress Layer 2 differentials again if supervision premium not indexed.

Technology & Authority Implications for Layer 2

  • Centralized vs. decentralized: ESL, AI cameras, Facewatch centralize decisions. Remote tablets decentralize authority. Tension between human oversight and automation.
  • Audit trail opacity: Algorithm-driven decisions mean supervisor cannot explain "why." Compliance risk if challenged.
  • Liability allocation: Unclear who is liable when technology fails. Retailers push liability to vendors; supervisors caught in middle.
  • Training gap: New systems deployed with minimal Layer 2 training. Expected to troubleshoot without deep understanding.
  • Privacy & data rights: Supervisors often unaware of data collection. Personal GDPR rights not clearly communicated.
